# Narrative Frameworks Reference
## NYC DOT Sidewalk Inspection & Management — Data Storytelling

---

## When to use each framework

| Framework | Best for | NYC DOT example |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Situation–Complication–Resolution (SCR)** | Problem/solution, escalation briefs, budget requests | "Brooklyn ramp completion has declined — here is why and what we need to do" |
| **What–So What–Now What (WSWNW)** | Status updates, findings briefings, committee reports | "Violation rates are up 23% — here is what that means and what should happen next" |
| **Before–After–Bridge (BAB)** | Change stories, impact reports, program updates | "Before the inspector reallocation: 58% completion. After: 72%. Here is how we sustain it." |

---

## Framework 1: Situation–Complication–Resolution (SCR)

Most common and most effective for operational briefings. Mirrors how decisions are made: shared context → the problem → the answer.

### Structure

**SITUATION** — Establish common ground. State facts your audience already accepts.
- 1–2 sentences maximum.
- No new information here; this is context-setting only.
- DOT example: "NYC DOT manages the SIM program across ~398K inspection records, with ADA ramp commitments in all five boroughs."

**COMPLICATION** — Introduce the tension or problem. This is the "but then..." moment.
- Lead with the central finding immediately. Do not bury it.
- Quantify: percentage change, absolute gap from target, affected population.
- DOT example: "Brooklyn's ramp completion rate dropped from 74% to 62% in Q2 2026 — the largest single-quarter decline in three years — affecting an estimated 8,400 residents who rely on accessible routes on those blocks."

**RESOLUTION** — The clear path forward. Answer-first.
- State the recommendation before the supporting rationale.
- Specify: what to do, who is responsible, by when.
- DOT example: "Reallocating 15 inspector-weeks to Brooklyn through August, combined with priority scheduling for 120 overdue ramp orders, will restore the completion rate to 72% by Q3 end."

### Common mistakes with SCR

- **Situation is too long:** If your situation section has new information or takes more than 2 sentences, compress it. The audience already knows the context.
- **Complication is buried:** Never save the key finding for the end. State it in the first sentence of the Complication.
- **Resolution is vague:** "We need more resources" is not a resolution. "We need 15 inspector-weeks and $180K in overtime, approved by July 15" is.

## Framework 2: What–So What–Now What (WSWNW)

Best for findings that need interpretation — where the data is clear but the implications are not. Common in monthly briefings and committee reports.

### Structure

**WHAT** — The factual finding, stated neutrally.
- No interpretation yet — just what the data shows.
- Include: metric, value, period, comparison basis, data source.
- DOT example: "The citywide ramp completion rate stands at 67% as of June 2026, compared to a 75% program target."

**SO WHAT** — Why does this finding matter to this audience?
- Bridge from data to operational or business consequence.
- Different audiences have different "so whats" — tailor by role.
- Leadership SO WHAT: "Missing the 75% target by 8 pp means 3,200 ramps remain inaccessible, increasing ADA grievance risk and 311 complaint volume."
- Council SO WHAT: "In District 45, 47 ramps on the capital commitment list remain incomplete — constituents on [STREET] and [STREET] are affected."
- Ops Manager SO WHAT: "At current inspection velocity, Queens will need 22 additional inspector-weeks before September to close the gap."

**NOW WHAT** — The concrete recommended action.
- Specific, owned, time-bound. No vague language.
- DOT example: "By July 15, the Brooklyn Borough Manager should submit a revised Q3 inspection schedule prioritising the 120 overdue ramp orders, for Commissioner approval."

## Framework 3: Before–After–Bridge (BAB)

Best for demonstrating change — showing impact of a program, intervention, or policy. Effective for annual reports and progress briefings.

### Structure

**BEFORE** — The problem or baseline state.
- Use specific historical metrics with dates.
- DOT example: "In Q1 2026, Brooklyn's completion rate stood at 58%, with 340 ramps in backlog and a data quality score of 62/100."

**AFTER** — The current state following the change.
- Lead with the change, quantified.
- DOT example: "After the Q2 inspector reallocation and targeted scheduling effort, Brooklyn's completion rate reached 74% — a 16-percentage-point gain — with backlog reduced to 89 ramps."

**BRIDGE** — Why did it change, and what comes next?
- Explain the mechanism (what drove the improvement or decline).
- Set up the next decision or action.
- DOT example: "The gain was driven by scheduling ramp-only inspection days and reducing non-productive travel time by 30%. Sustaining this requires continued prioritised scheduling through Q4, and a staffing review before the winter slowdown."

## Emotional Arc Design

Every narrative should move the audience through an emotional progression:

| Stage | Target emotion | How to achieve it |
|-------|--------------|------------------|
| **Opening** | Recognition / comfort | Start with shared context they already know and agree with |
| **Complication** | Concern / urgency | State the problem with concrete numbers — avoid vague language like "significant" |
| **Resolution** | Confidence / relief | Give a specific, actionable recommendation they can actually execute |
| **Close** | Commitment | Name the decision owner and deadline — make the call to action concrete |

### Opening techniques (capture attention in < 10 seconds)

- **Striking number:** "6,200 NYC residents cannot safely access a sidewalk ramp within one block of their home."
- **Counterintuitive finding:** "Despite a record inspection budget, ramp completion is at a 3-year low in three of five boroughs."
- **Direct question:** "How many additional inspectors does it take to close Brooklyn's gap before the fiscal year ends?"
- **Humanised impact:** "Residents on [STREET] in [NEIGHBOURHOOD] have been waiting 18 months for an ADA ramp promised in a 2024 capital commitment."

### Closing techniques (drive commitment)

- State the decision, decision owner, and deadline explicitly.
- Give the cost of inaction in concrete terms.
- End on one clear ask — not three.
